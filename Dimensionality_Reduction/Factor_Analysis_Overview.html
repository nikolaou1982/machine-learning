Factor Analysis Overview Guide

Factor Analysis: identifies latent factors that explain correlations between observed variables.

Primary Uses

Latent factor identification, dimensionality reduction, correlation structure analysis, psychometric testing, social science research, data interpretation, reducing observed variables to underlying constructs.

Strengths

Models underlying latent factors, explains correlations between variables, interpretable factors, handles measurement error, provides factor loadings, useful for theory-driven analysis.

Watch-outs

Requires factor rotation for interpretability, number of factors must be specified, assumes linear relationships, sensitive to sample size, factor interpretation can be subjective, may not converge.

How It Works Mathematical Foundation

Factor Analysis models observed variables as linear combinations of latent factors plus error. It identifies underlying factors that explain correlations between observed variables, making it useful for understanding the structure of data.

  • Factor Model: X = ΛF + ε where X is observed variables, Λ is factor loadings, F is latent factors, ε is unique error
  • Covariance Structure: Σ = ΛΦΛᵀ + Ψ where Σ is observed covariance, Φ is factor covariance, Ψ is unique variance
  • Factor Extraction: Use Principal Factor Analysis (PFA) or Maximum Likelihood (ML) to estimate loadings
  • Factor Rotation: Apply Varimax, Oblimin, or other rotations to improve interpretability
  • Factor Loadings: Λᵢⱼ indicates how much variable i loads on factor j (correlation between variable and factor)
  • Communality: h²ᵢ = Σⱼ λ²ᵢⱼ is proportion of variance in variable i explained by factors
Tip: Factor Analysis focuses on explaining correlations between variables through latent factors, while PCA focuses on maximizing variance. Factor Analysis is more theory-driven and interpretable.

Reading the Visualizations Analysis

  • Factor Loadings Plot: Shows how each variable loads on each factor. High absolute loadings (|λ| > 0.3-0.4) indicate strong relationship between variable and factor.
  • Scree Plot: Shows eigenvalues of correlation matrix. Look for elbow to determine number of factors. Factors after elbow explain little variance.
  • Communality: Shows proportion of each variable's variance explained by factors. High communality (>0.6) indicates variable is well-explained by factors.
  • Factor Correlation: Shows correlations between factors (if oblique rotation). Orthogonal factors are uncorrelated.
  • Factor Scores: Shows data projected onto factor space. Similar to PCA scores but based on factor model.

Parameter Guidance Best Practice

  • Number of Factors: Use Kaiser criterion (eigenvalues > 1), scree plot elbow, or parallel analysis. Can also specify based on theory.
  • Factor Rotation: Varimax (orthogonal) for uncorrelated factors, Oblimin/Promax (oblique) for correlated factors. Rotation improves interpretability.
  • Extraction Method: Principal Factor Analysis (PFA) for exploratory, Maximum Likelihood (ML) for confirmatory. ML provides fit statistics.
  • Minimum Loading: Typically consider |λ| > 0.3-0.4 as meaningful. Lower thresholds may include noise.
  • Communality Threshold: Variables with low communality (<0.4) may not fit the factor model well. Consider removing or investigating.

Data Preparation Best Practice

  • Correlation Matrix: Factor Analysis works on correlation matrix. Standardize variables (mean=0, std=1) or use correlation matrix directly.
  • Sample Size: Need sufficient samples (typically n > 100, ideally n > 200) for stable factor estimates. More samples = more reliable factors.
  • Missing Values: Remove or impute missing values. Factor Analysis cannot handle NaN directly.
  • Outliers: Extreme outliers can distort correlations. Consider robust methods or outlier removal.
  • Multicollinearity: Highly correlated variables (r > 0.9) may cause issues. Consider removing redundant variables.
  • Normality: Maximum Likelihood assumes multivariate normality. For non-normal data, use Principal Factor Analysis.

Common Pitfalls Avoid

  • Too Many Factors: Over-extraction leads to factors that explain noise. Use scree plot and Kaiser criterion to guide selection.
  • Too Few Factors: Under-extraction may miss important factors. Check if communalities are reasonable (>0.5 on average).
  • No Rotation: Unrotated factors are often hard to interpret. Always apply rotation (Varimax, Oblimin, etc.) for clarity.
  • Ignoring Communality: Low communality variables don't fit the factor model. Investigate or remove them.
  • Small Sample Size: With n < 100, factor estimates may be unstable. Need larger samples for reliable results.
  • Interpreting Loadings as Causal: Factor loadings show correlations, not causation. Don't assume causal relationships.
  • Confusing with PCA: Factor Analysis models latent factors; PCA maximizes variance. Use Factor Analysis for theory-driven analysis.

When to Use Application

  • Latent Factor Identification: When you want to identify underlying constructs that explain correlations between observed variables.
  • Theory-Driven Analysis: When you have hypotheses about underlying factors and want to test them.
  • Psychometric Testing: When developing or validating psychological tests, questionnaires, or scales.
  • Social Science Research: When analyzing survey data, attitudes, behaviors, or other social constructs.
  • Dimensionality Reduction: When you want to reduce many observed variables to fewer latent factors while preserving correlation structure.
  • Data Interpretation: When you want to understand what underlying factors drive correlations in your data.
  • Measurement Model: When you want to model how observed variables relate to latent constructs.

Alternatives to Consider Comparison

  • vs PCA: Factor Analysis models latent factors explaining correlations; PCA maximizes variance. Use Factor Analysis for theory-driven analysis, PCA for variance maximization.
  • vs Independent Component Analysis (ICA): Factor Analysis finds correlated factors; ICA finds independent sources. Use ICA when you want statistically independent components.
  • vs Structural Equation Modeling (SEM): Factor Analysis is exploratory; SEM is confirmatory with causal paths. Use SEM for testing causal models.
  • vs Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) vs Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA): EFA explores factor structure; CFA tests hypothesized structure. Use EFA for exploration, CFA for hypothesis testing.
